How the Japanese Name Converter Works

Our Japanese name converter uses phonetic transliteration to convert your English name into three Japanese writing systems. When you enter your name, the tool maps each English syllable to its closest Japanese phonetic equivalent, following standard Hepburn romanization rules used in Japan for foreign names.

Foreign names in Japan are traditionally written in Katakana (カタカナ), the syllabary specifically designed for loanwords, foreign names, and onomatopoeia. Our converter also generates Hiragana (ひらがな) and Kanji (漢字) representations using ateji — kanji characters selected for their phonetic readings rather than semantic meaning.

Understanding Japanese Phonetics for Names

Japanese uses a mora-based phonetic system rather than syllables. Each mora represents one rhythmic beat — for example, "ka" (カ) is one mora, while the long vowel in "kā" (カー) counts as two morae. When converting English names, certain sounds that don't exist in Japanese are adapted: "L" becomes "R" (ル), "V" becomes "B" (ブ), and consonant clusters are broken with vowels. The name "Smith" becomes スミス (Su-mi-su) because Japanese doesn't allow consonant clusters.

Japanese honorific suffixes like -san (さん), -chan (ちゃん), -kun (くん), and -sama (さま) are commonly added after names to show respect or familiarity. These naming conventions are an important part of Japanese culture and social hierarchy.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I write my name in Japanese?
Foreign names are written in Katakana in Japan. Enter your name in our converter above, and you'll receive your name in Katakana, Hiragana, and Kanji. Katakana is the officially recognized script for foreign names in Japanese documents, passports, and official records.
What is the difference between Katakana, Hiragana, and Kanji?
Katakana (カタカナ) uses angular strokes and is for foreign words and names. Hiragana (ひらがな) uses curved strokes for native Japanese words and grammar. Kanji (漢字) are logographic characters from Chinese, each representing a meaning. For foreign names, Katakana is the standard and most appropriate choice.
Is this Japanese name converter accurate?
Our converter uses standard phonetic transliteration rules based on Hepburn romanization, the most widely used system in Japan. For common English names, we use a verified database of established conversions. For less common names, our algorithm applies Japanese phonetic mapping rules to produce the closest natural-sounding equivalent.
Can I use the Kanji version for a tattoo?
The Kanji output uses ateji — characters chosen for sound rather than meaning. While visually striking, we recommend consulting a native Japanese speaker or professional calligrapher before getting a permanent kanji tattoo, to ensure the character combination is appropriate and culturally respectful. Katakana is always a safe and authentic choice for foreign name tattoos.
Does this work for names from other languages?
Yes! Our tool works as an English to Japanese name converter, and can also handle names from Spanish, French, German, Korean, Chinese, and other languages that use the Latin alphabet. The converter analyzes the phonetic structure of your name regardless of its origin language.
Why does my name sound different in Japanese?
Japanese phonetics don't include certain English sounds. There is no "L" sound (it becomes "R"), no "V" (becomes "B"), and most syllables must end in a vowel or "n." Consonant clusters are separated with vowels. This is why "Clark" becomes クラーク (Ku-rā-ku) — each consonant gets a vowel companion to fit Japanese mora structure.
